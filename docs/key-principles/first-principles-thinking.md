# First Principles Thinking

When faced with a complex problem, most people reason by analogy — they look at
what others have done and adapt it. First principles thinking takes the opposite
approach: it decomposes the problem down to its most fundamental truths and
reasons upward from there. This produces solutions that are grounded in reality
rather than inherited assumptions.

**This document is written in Minto Pyramid format.**

---

## Governing Thought

First principles thinking is a reasoning framework that strips a problem back to
its foundational truths — the irreducible facts that cannot be broken down
further — and builds understanding and solutions upward from those truths. It is
the antidote to reasoning by analogy, convention, or authority.

---

## Situation

Every day, teams make decisions: what to build, how to build it, which
trade-offs to accept. Most of these decisions are made by analogy — "other teams
do it this way", "the framework recommends this pattern", "we have always done
it like that". Analogical reasoning is efficient: it leverages existing
knowledge and avoids re-deriving everything from scratch.

## Complication

Analogical reasoning fails when the analogy is wrong, when the context has
changed, or when the inherited assumption was never correct in the first place.
Teams end up defending practices they cannot justify, building on foundations
they have never examined, and solving the wrong problem because nobody asked
whether the problem statement itself was valid.

## Question

How should a team reason about problems when existing assumptions may be wrong,
when the problem itself may be mis-stated, or when a genuinely novel solution is
needed?

## Key-Line 1: The Philosophical Foundation

### Origin in Aristotle

First principles thinking originates with **Aristotle**, who defined a first
principle as:

> "A first principle is the first basis from which a thing is known."
>
> — Aristotle, *Metaphysics*, Book V, 1013a

Aristotle distinguished between knowledge derived from first principles
(episteme) and knowledge derived from experience or convention (empeiria). He
argued that true understanding requires tracing knowledge back to propositions
that are self-evidently true and cannot be deduced from anything more
fundamental.

### The Cartesian Method

**René Descartes** formalised a systematic approach in his *Discourse on the
Method* (1637):

1. **Accept nothing as true** that is not clearly known to be so.
2. **Divide** each problem into as many parts as possible.
3. **Build upward** from the simplest and most certain truths.
4. **Review** thoroughly to ensure nothing is omitted.

> "I would accept nothing as true which I did not clearly recognise to be so
> ... and to divide each of the difficulties under examination into as many
> parts as possible."
>
> — René Descartes, *Discourse on the Method* (1637), Part II

This is, in essence, the first principles method expressed as a procedure.

## Key-Line 2: The Modern Application

### Elon Musk and SpaceX

The most frequently cited modern example is **Elon Musk's** approach to rocket
cost at SpaceX:

> "I tend to approach things from a physics framework. Physics teaches you to
> reason from first principles rather than by analogy. So I said, okay, let's
> look at the first principles. What is a rocket made of? Aerospace-grade
> aluminium alloys, plus some titanium, copper, and carbon fibre. Then I asked,
> what is the value of those materials on the commodity market? It turned out
> that the materials cost of a rocket was around two percent of the typical
> price."
>
> — Elon Musk, interview with Kevin Rose (2012)

Rather than accepting that rockets cost what they cost (reasoning by analogy
with existing aerospace pricing), Musk decomposed the question to the material
constituents and their commodity prices — the first principles — and built a new
cost model from there. This led to SpaceX manufacturing most components
in-house, dramatically reducing costs.

### Charlie Munger and Mental Models

**Charlie Munger**, vice-chairman of Berkshire Hathaway, advocates for what he
calls "elementary, worldly wisdom" — a latticework of mental models drawn from
fundamental disciplines (physics, mathematics, psychology, economics). First
principles reasoning is the foundational method:

> "You must know the big ideas in the big disciplines, and use them routinely —
> all of them, not just a few."
>
> — Charlie Munger, *Poor Charlie's Almanack* (2005)

Munger's approach treats first principles as the atomic building blocks of a
mental model toolkit. Rather than reasoning from a single domain's conventions,
he reasons from the fundamental truths of multiple disciplines simultaneously.

## Key-Line 3: The Method in Practice

### Step 1 — Identify and Challenge Assumptions

Begin by listing every assumption that underpins the current understanding of
the problem. For each assumption, ask:

- **Is this definitely true, or is it merely conventional?**
- **Where did this assumption come from?**
- **What would change if this assumption were wrong?**

### Step 2 — Ask "Why?" — Including Why the Question Exists

First principles thinking does not start with the proposed solution or even with
the problem as stated. It starts one level deeper:

- **Why is this question being asked?**
- **What is the actual outcome someone needs?**
- **Is the stated problem the real problem, or a symptom of something else?**

This is critical. Many problems are mis-stated because the person asking has
already made assumptions about the shape of the answer. By questioning the
question itself, the thinker may discover that the real problem is entirely
different.

### Step 3 — Decompose to Fundamental Truths

Break the problem down until you reach statements that are:

- **Self-evidently true** — they do not depend on any other assumption.
- **Empirically verifiable** — they can be tested or measured.
- **Irreducible** — they cannot be broken down further.

These are the first principles.

### Step 4 — Reason Upward

From the first principles, build the solution or understanding step by step.
Each step must follow logically from the previous one. If a step requires an
assumption, that assumption must itself be traceable to a first principle or be
explicitly acknowledged as an assumption.

### Step 5 — Validate Against Reality

Test the conclusion against observable reality. If it does not hold, one or more
of the supposed first principles may not actually be fundamental, or the
reasoning chain may contain an error. Return to Step 3 and refine.

```text
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│  Why is this question being     │
│  asked? What outcome is needed? │
└──────────────┬──────────────────┘
               ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│  Challenge every assumption     │
│  Is it true, or just convention?│
└──────────────┬──────────────────┘
               ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│  Decompose to irreducible       │
│  fundamental truths              │
└──────────────┬──────────────────┘
               ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│  Reason upward, step by step    │
└──────────────┬──────────────────┘
               ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│  Validate against reality       │
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Key-Line 4: First Principles vs. Reasoning by Analogy

| Dimension | First Principles | Reasoning by Analogy |
|---|---|---|
| **Starting point** | Fundamental truths | What others have done |
| **Speed** | Slower — requires decomposition | Faster — reuses existing patterns |
| **Risk of error** | Lower when done rigorously | Higher — the analogy may not hold |
| **Innovation potential** | High — can produce novel solutions | Low — produces variations of existing solutions |
| **When to use** | Novel problems, high stakes, inherited assumptions seem wrong | Familiar problems, low stakes, existing solutions are well-proven |
| **Failure mode** | Over-analysis, impractical in time-constrained settings | Cargo-culting, blind adherence to convention |

Both modes of reasoning are valuable. First principles thinking is not a
replacement for analogical reasoning — it is a complement, to be used when
analogical reasoning is insufficient or when the problem demands genuine novelty.

## Key-Line 5: Application in Software Engineering

### Architecture Decisions

When choosing an architecture (microservices vs. modular monolith, event
sourcing vs. CRUD, SQL vs. NoSQL), first principles thinking asks:

- What are the actual consistency requirements? (Not "what does the industry
  say" — what does **this system** actually need?)
- What are the actual scale requirements? (Not "we might need to scale" — what
  is the measured or projected load?)
- What are the actual team constraints? (Not "microservices are best practice"
  — can this team operate independent deployments?)

### Process Decisions

When designing a team process, first principles thinking asks:

- Why do we have this ceremony? What outcome does it produce?
- If we removed it, what would we lose? Would we notice?
- Is the process serving the team, or is the team serving the process?

### Debugging

First principles debugging decomposes the symptom:

- What is the exact observable behaviour?
- What are the possible causes, given the known system state?
- Which cause is most consistent with the evidence?

This is the scientific method applied to software defects.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Description |
|---|---|
| **Analysis paralysis** | Decomposing endlessly without reaching actionable conclusions. Set a time box. |
| **False first principles** | Treating an assumption as a first principle when it is not actually irreducible. Always ask "can this be broken down further?" |
| **Ignoring context** | First principles in physics are universal; in business and software, context matters. What is fundamental in one organisation may not be in another. |
| **Dismissing all analogy** | Not every decision needs first-principles analysis. Use it when the stakes are high or when existing approaches are clearly failing. |
| **Forgetting to ask why the question exists** | The most common and most costly pitfall. If you do not question the question, you may solve the wrong problem perfectly. |

## Summary

First principles thinking decomposes a problem to its irreducible fundamental
truths and reasons upward from there. It originates with Aristotle, was
formalised by Descartes, and has been applied successfully in modern business and
engineering by practitioners such as Elon Musk and Charlie Munger. The method
has five steps: question why the question is being asked, challenge assumptions,
decompose to fundamentals, reason upward, and validate against reality. It is
most valuable when existing conventions are failing, when the problem is novel,
or when the stakes demand genuine understanding rather than inherited patterns.
